Introduction

This website explores the crucial intersection between the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and the development of academic literacy within higher education institutions. We delve into how fostering robust academic literacy skills can significantly contribute to achieving the ambitious targets set by the SDGs, particularly in preparing students to address global challenges.

UN Sustainable Development Goals

The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), also known as the Global Goals, were adopted by the United Nations in 2015 as a universal call to action to end poverty, protect the planet, and ensure that by 2030 all people enjoy peace and prosperity. These 17 integrated goals recognize that action in one area will affect outcomes in others, and that development must balance social, economic and environmental sustainability.

The 17 SDGs are:

  • No Poverty
  • Zero Hunger
  • Good Health and Well-being
  • Quality Education
  • Gender Equality
  • Clean Water and Sanitation
  • Affordable and Clean Energy
  • Decent Work and Economic Growth
  • Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure
  • Reduced Inequalities
  • Sustainable Cities and Communities
  • Responsible Consumption and Production
  • Climate Action
  • Life Below Water
  • Life on Land
  • Peace, Justice, and Strong Institutions
  • Partnerships for the Goals

Academic Literacy: Horizontal and Vertical Knowledge

Academic literacy, as theorized by Basil Bernstein, is essential for navigating the complex knowledge structures prevalent in higher education. It distinguishes between two primary forms of knowledge:

Horizontal Knowledge

Horizontal knowledge refers to segmented, context-specific knowledge domains. It is often learned informally, rooted in specific social or cultural experiences, and tends to be oral, culturally embedded, and contextually based. Examples include indigenous knowledge systems and everyday social interactions.

Vertical Knowledge

Vertical knowledge, in contrast, is coherent, systematically principled knowledge. It consists of integrated bodies of knowledge that build cumulatively, organized through generalized rules and methodologies. This form of knowledge is specialized and symbolic (e.g., physical sciences), with a hierarchical organization and explicit validation rules, facilitating progression and conceptual understanding.

The challenge for students, particularly in diverse educational contexts, lies in transitioning from their familiar horizontal knowledge systems to the vertical knowledge structures demanded by higher education. Academic literacy developers play a critical mediating role in bridging this gap, helping students master the reading, writing, reasoning, and argumentation styles specific to academic disciplines.

The Link: SDGs and Academic Literacy

The development of academic literacy is intrinsically linked to the achievement of the UN SDGs. Higher education, through its focus on vertical knowledge structures, equips students with the critical thinking, research, and communication skills necessary to understand and address complex global challenges.

  • Quality Education (SDG 4): Academic literacy is a cornerstone of quality education. By developing strong reading, writing, and critical analysis skills, students are better prepared to engage with complex information, conduct research, and contribute to knowledge creation, directly supporting SDG 4.
  • Reduced Inequalities (SDG 10): Inclusive academic literacy practices, especially those adopting an African-centered approach, can bridge the gap between diverse knowledge systems (horizontal and vertical). This empowers students from varied backgrounds to succeed in higher education, reducing educational inequalities and fostering a more equitable society.
  • Peace, Justice, and Strong Institutions (SDG 16): Academic literacy enables students to critically analyze societal issues, engage in informed debate, and propose evidence-based solutions. These skills are vital for building strong institutions and promoting peaceful, just societies.
  • Climate Action (SDG 13) & Other Environmental Goals: Addressing complex issues like climate change requires advanced scientific literacy and the ability to synthesize information from various disciplines. Academic literacy provides the tools for students to engage with scientific literature, communicate findings effectively, and advocate for sustainable practices.
  • Partnerships for the Goals (SDG 17): Effective collaboration and communication are essential for achieving the SDGs. Academic literacy, particularly in its emphasis on clear and persuasive communication, facilitates interdisciplinary and international partnerships aimed at solving global problems.

Conclusion

The synergy between academic literacy development and the UN Sustainable Development Goals is undeniable. Higher education plays a pivotal role in cultivating the intellectual capacities and critical skills necessary for individuals to contribute meaningfully to global sustainability efforts. By prioritizing inclusive and effective academic literacy programs, educational institutions can empower a new generation of leaders and innovators to tackle the world's most pressing challenges and realize the vision of the SDGs.
